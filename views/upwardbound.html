What is Educational Equity?

Educational equity means ensuring that all students, regardless of their background, have the opportunity to succeed academically and socially.

SCOPP

A Pittsburgh-based program that gave academically overlooked students the chance to pursue college by boosting their skills, confidence, and support.

Upward Bound

A federal initiative inspired by SCOPP, expanding access to higher education for low-income and first-generation students nationwide.

Why This Matters

SCOPP and Upward Bound addressed historic inequities during the Civil Rights Era and continue to shape access to education today.

Upward Bound

Introduction

Founded in 1965 as a part of President Lyndon B. Johnson’s War on Poverty, Upward Bound became the first of the federal TRIO programs. It was created to help high school students from low-income families and those whose parents did not attend college to prepare for postsecondary education. Modeled in part after SCOPP, Upward Bound scaled the mission of educational equity nationwide, offering academic enrichment, mentoring, and college readiness support to students who had long been excluded from opportunity.

The Experience

Founded in 1965, Upward Bound was created to provide sustained, year-round academic and emotional support for high school students from low-income and first-generation college families. Modeled in part after SCOPP, Upward Bound expanded the mission from a local experiment to a national commitment- empowering students across the country to envision themselves in college and beyond.

  • Operates throughout the academic year and summer, offering continuous support to students starting in the 9th or 10th grade.
  • Provides instruction in core academic subjects such as English, Mathematics, Science, and Foreign Language, along with college readiness skills.
  • Offers tutoring, mentoring, personal and career counseling, and assistance with SAT/ACT prep and financial aid applications.
  • Includes a summer residential component hosted on a college campus, where students experience college life, take classes, and engage with peers from diverse backgrounds.
  • Encourages cultural enrichment through trips to museums, performances, and college tours.
  • Focuses on long-term success: students are followed through high school graduation and supported during the college application and enrollment process.
  • Funded by the U.S. Department of Education and hosted at hundreds of colleges and universities across the United States.

The Participants

Upward Bound was created to serve high school students who face significant barriers to higher education—specifically those from low-income families and students who would be the first in their families to attend college. From the beginning, the program was designed to recognize potential where traditional systems had failed to look for it.

How are students selected?

Unlike programs that rely solely on academic records or standardized test scores, Upward Bound identifies students based on financial eligibility and first-generation college status, alongside academic need. Students are often nominated by counselors or apply directly, demonstrating ambition, curiosity, and a desire to pursue higher education—even if they lack formal preparation.

Each program site conducts its own interview process, which may involve students and their families. This approach centers the student’s voice and life context, helping educators understand the obstacles they’ve faced and the strengths they bring. Many participants share that Upward Bound was the first time they felt truly seen by the education system.

Demographic Impact

Today, Upward Bound serves more than 60,000 students annually through programs housed at colleges and universities nationwide. The vast majority of participants are students of color, students from low-income households, and students attending under-resourced schools. The program’s enduring mission is rooted in a belief that college success should not be limited by a student’s zip code, background, or family history.

“Upward Bound gave me the tools and confidence to believe I was college material.” – Upward Bound Alumnus

Participation in Upward Bound is more than academic—it’s transformational. The program helps students define their futures on their own terms, building not just transcripts, but identity, purpose, and community. In doing so, it continues the work started by SCOPP and other early equity initiatives—expanding what college readiness truly means.

Historical Lens

Upward Bound was born in the shadow of landmark legislation and urgent social change. Established in 1965 as part of President Lyndon B. Johnson’s War on Poverty, the program reflected a growing national realization that access to higher education was not equally distributed—and that deep inequalities rooted in race, class, and geography had long gone unaddressed.

While the Civil Rights Act of 1964 outlawed segregation in schools, the reality on the ground was different. Many public schools serving Black, Latinx, and low-income students remained underfunded, overcrowded, and overlooked. Higher education—particularly elite institutions—remained largely out of reach, not due to a lack of student ability, but due to entrenched barriers in opportunity, guidance, and resources.

Upward Bound took a bold step forward: it redefined college access not as something earned through privilege, but something supported through **investment and intention**. The program created a national structure that prioritized academic support, cultural exposure, and long-term mentorship, giving students the tools to succeed and a sense of belonging in institutions that had often excluded them.

“We must open the doors of opportunity. But we must also equip our people to walk through those doors.” – Lyndon B. Johnson, 1964

What began as an experimental federal initiative quickly became the cornerstone of the **TRIO programs**, which continue today as a multi-pronged strategy to promote educational equity. Upward Bound’s legacy lies not only in how many students it has served—but in how it helped transform national ideas of who could go to college, and why. It is a story of systems shifting toward inclusion—and of students claiming space that was long denied to them.

Linguistic Lens

Language matters—especially when it comes to how we talk about students. In the early years of Upward Bound, much of the language mirrored the deficit-based terms common in the 1960s. Participants were described as “disadvantaged,” “at-risk,” or from “problem backgrounds”—phrasing that focused more on perceived lack than on potential. These labels, though often used with good intentions, subtly framed students as passive recipients of charity rather than active, capable learners. (1)

However, even in early coverage and program reports, we can see efforts to challenge that framing. Coordinators and students alike began emphasizing **growth**, **resilience**, and **motivation**—words that signaled a shift from labeling students by their limitations to recognizing their ambition and drive. In a 1966 *Washington Post* feature, students were described as “eager,” “hopeful,” and “college-bound,” highlighting the belief that with proper support, their academic futures were not only possible, but expected. (2)

This linguistic shift was significant. Upward Bound became one of the first national programs to **redefine success not as innate privilege, but as nurtured potential**. The language surrounding students moved from a lens of remediation to one of recognition: not “fixing” students, but equipping them. This shift continues today, as Upward Bound staff use asset-based language that centers students' strengths, aspirations, and cultural backgrounds. Words became tools for empowerment—and for many students, the program was the first place where they were described not as behind, but as bound for greatness.

Conclusion

Upward Bound has become one of the most enduring educational equity programs in the United States. Since its founding in 1965, it has supported hundreds of thousands of students in becoming first-generation college graduates—students who might have been overlooked by the system, but were empowered by opportunity. The program’s reach goes beyond college admissions; it transforms confidence, identity, and entire life trajectories.

As the first of the federal TRIO programs, Upward Bound built a framework for long-term academic support that continues to this day. It proved that with consistent guidance, mentorship, and academic enrichment, students from any background can thrive in higher education. Its success also validated the idea that college readiness is not a product of privilege—but of support, belief, and community investment.

In conclusion, Upward Bound continues the work SCOPP started—reshaping what it means to prepare for college and who gets to access it. It reminds us that achieving equity in education requires not only removing barriers, but actively building bridges. Upward Bound does exactly that: it walks alongside students, helping them imagine and pursue futures once deemed out of reach.
